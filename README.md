 # Extreme Disaster Events in Southern Africa (2000–2026)

## Overview
Southern Africa has experienced a significant increase in extreme weather events such as droughts, floods, and cyclones over the past two decades. This project examines how the frequency and intensity of these disaster events have changed across Southern Africa from 2000 to 2026. Using EM-DAT disaster data and UN SDG 13 climate indicators, it explores which countries in the region have been most affected and how disaster-related deaths and displacement have evolved over time. The goal is to visualize these trends in a way that helps communities and policymakers understand the urgency of climate action.

---

## Key Findings
- 575 natural disasters recorded across 10 Southern African countries (2000–2026)
- Floods (226 events) and Storms (143 events) are the most frequent disaster types
- 2008 was the deadliest year with 5,385 deaths — largely driven by epidemic outbreaks
- 2022 and 2023 were among the most deadly recent years with 3,849 and 3,561 deaths respectively
- Malawi (33M), South Africa (31M) and Zimbabwe (30M) had the highest number of people affected
- Mozambique and Madagascar are most frequently impacted by tropical cyclones
- Droughts remain a persistent threat, particularly in Zimbabwe, Zambia and Botswana
